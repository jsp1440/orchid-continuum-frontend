# Living Homepage Philosophy

## Purpose

The public Orchid Continuum homepage is not a catalog of features. It is a guided journey through orchid life and the relationships that make orchid conservation, education, research, and cultivation possible.

The homepage should help visitors understand one central idea:

> The world of orchids is complex. The Orchid Continuum makes the path to understanding it clearer.

## Core narrative

The homepage should answer these questions in sequence:

1. **Wonder** — What am I looking at?
2. **Diversity** — What does this Featured Genus contain?
3. **Relationships** — What connects these orchids to the rest of life?
4. **Need** — Why does the Orchid Continuum exist?
5. **Exploration** — What can I discover here?
6. **Conservation** — Why does this matter?
7. **Participation** — How can I return, learn, contribute, or help?

No homepage section should repeat the same message in different words. Every section should answer a different scientific or human question.

## Featured Genus principle

The organizing unit is the **Featured Genus**, not Species of the Day.

Reason:

- There are hundreds of orchid genera and tens of thousands of species.
- Featuring a genus allows visitors to compare species within a lineage.
- It reveals what species share and how they differ.
- It supports rotating images and stories without losing narrative coherence.

Operating model:

- A Featured Genus changes on a 12-hour cadence.
- Species within that genus rotate through the hero/gallery experience on a shorter cadence, such as 45 seconds.
- Captions, relationship cards, habitat context, pollinators, fungi, maps, and evidence should update with the active species where data exists.

## The orchid is the hero

The first public impression should be a living orchid, not a logo.

The logo should remain visible as the institutional mark, ideally in the header or upper-left identity position. It should not compete with the orchid image.

The homepage should begin with beauty, then reveal science, relationships, and conservation.

## Knowledge graph principle

The knowledge graph should not merely display a diagram. It should teach what connected knowledge empowers people to do.

Each spoke should function as a doorway into a question:

- Who pollinates it?
- What fungi support it?
- Where does it live?
- When does it flower?
- What does literature say?
- What is unknown?
- What conservation actions follow?

A visitor should leave understanding that a knowledge graph helps reveal relationships that are otherwise hidden across disconnected databases, papers, images, maps, and observations.

## Homepage Atlas principle

The homepage Atlas should be simple, thematic, and beautiful. It should not behave like a full GIS workstation.

Homepage thematic map ideas:

- Flowering through time
- Pollinator distributions
- Mycorrhizal associations
- Habitat
- Elevation
- Rainfall
- Temperature
- Protected areas
- Conservation projects
- Knowledge gaps
- Expedition opportunities

Advanced GIS controls belong in the Research Center or full Atlas workspace.

## Pollinator and mycorrhizal principle

Pollinators and fungi are not labels. They are organisms and evidence nodes.

Pollinator cards should eventually include images, taxonomy, distribution maps, migration when relevant, orchid associations, videos, literature, and knowledge graph links.

Mycorrhizal cards should eventually include fungal identity, fruiting bodies where known, microscopic imagery such as pelotons in orchid root cells, distribution maps, orchid hosts, other ecological associations, sequences, literature, and knowledge gaps.

## Calyx principle

Calyx is the guide, not an intrusive chatbot.

The public experience should not ask visitors to identify themselves up front or type complex information before trust is earned. Calyx should be available through a native Orchid Continuum interface, introduced as a research guide or curator.

Calyx should understand page context. If a visitor is viewing the Featured Genus, Calyx should know that context and offer relevant help.

There are two faces of Calyx:

- **Public Calyx** — guide, docent, explainer, and navigator.
- **Mission Control Calyx** — operator, scheduler, constitutional planner, and institutional steward.

## Footer and institutional access

Mission Control, Partners, Research Center, Governance, Constitution, API, Data Sources, Orchid University, and related institutional pathways should be discoverable without dominating the public tour.

The footer is the appropriate quiet home for these links.

## Return value

A successful homepage gives each audience a reason to return:

- Growers see habitat and cultivation insight.
- Students see understandable science.
- Teachers see educational pathways.
- Researchers see data integration and knowledge gaps.
- Conservationists see maps, relationships, and priorities.
- Orchid societies see community and learning.
- Curious visitors see beauty and story.

The desired emotional outcome is:

> There is a place for me here, and there will be something new to discover when I come back.
